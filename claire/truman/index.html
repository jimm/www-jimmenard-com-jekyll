Thesis

I believe that, taking into account the circumstances under which he became president, Harry Truman excelled as a leader who brought peace to the United States and Europe while keeping the American economy stable.

Becoming President

Harry Truman assumed the role of president within an unprecedented and very challenging set of circumstances. Truman, who originally served as Franklin Roosevelt’s vice president, was forced to assume the role of army chief upon Roosevelt’s untimely death only weeks after beginning his fourth and final term. Harry Truman never had any intention of running for president or taking on such an important role, but was sworn into office as soon as Roosevelt died. In a matter of days, Harry Truman had become the most important and influential man in America. Truman was given no time to prepare for his new position as president, and was not aware of many top secret projects going on at the time of his inauguration, including the development of the atomic bomb.

The Atomic Bomb

The dropping of the atomic bomb on Japan remains a highly controversial topic today. One could spend years pondering whether or not to make the decision to kill thousands in order to protect the lives of millions more. However, Truman only had three months to decide whether to drop the first bomb on Hiroshima. As vice president, Truman had been unaware of the development of the atomic bomb, and was only told of the project after it was put under his control when he became president. After almost no time, Truman was forced to make a decision that altered all of history. On August 6, 1945, Truman gave the order to drop the first bomb over Hiroshima, which he then announced to the American people twelve hours later. In his speech, Truman advocated the dropping of the bomb, saying it was the right thing to do. Truman said:

We are now prepared to obliterate more rapidly and completely every productive enterprise the Japanese have above ground in any city. We shall destroy their docks, their factories, and their communications. Let there be no mistake; we shall completely destroy Japan's power to make war.

It was to spare the Japanese people from utter destruction that the ultimatum of July 26 was issued at Potsdam. Their leaders promptly rejected that ultimatum. If they do not now accept our terms they may expect a rain of ruin from the air, the like of which has never been seen on this earth.

Truman was consistent in his defense of the decision to drop the bomb. This can be observed in a letter Truman wrote to his friend: “…it was done to save 125,000 youngsters on the American side and 125,000 on the Japanese side from getting killed and that is what it did. It probably also saved a half million youngsters on both sides from being maimed for life.” It is true that, in the short term, the effects of the atomic bomb were horrific. Hundreds of thousands were killed, and thousands more injured and left to die in awful ways. However, the dropping of the bomb on Hiroshima - and later on, Nagasaki - did cause Japan to surrender to America. In the long run, the bombings ultimately did save more lives than they took.

America's Economy

While WWII was still in progress, America’s economy boomed. Thousands of new jobs were created in the manufacturing of weapons, and women became more accepted in the workforce. During this time, Truman introduced his 21-point policy, called the Fair Deal. This policy addressed many important issues in America at the time, such as the rights of workers and aiding the educational system. Many of the points Truman proposed were considered liberal, in that they sought to aid small businesses and veterans. The Fair Deal, however, is mostly known for its proposed employment reforms. The Fair Deal, in trying to deal with unemployment compensation, tried to make the Fair Employment Practice Committee (which was also intended to address civil rights issues) permanent. Unfortunately, only a portion of the Fair Deal made it through Congress. Both national health insurance and educational assistance measures were voted down. Today, Truman’s Fair Deal policy is considered a mixed success. While some aspects of it thrived, such as the housing program and the expansion of social security, a government program people still use today, others did not succeed.

Civil Rights

But even when Truman did not fully succeed, his failures were easily drowned out by his other impressive accomplishments. Truman tapped into subjects other presidents did not, the biggest being civil rights. Truman sought to protect African Americans by law. He especially wanted to make lynching illegal and create a Civil Rights branch of the Department of Justice. Truman wanted to help other marginalized groups as well, such as women in the workforce. Truman tried to solve some of these inequalities through multiple points in his Fair Deal policy. In a speech given in 1948, Truman listed ten of his objectives to improve civil rights in America:

We believe that all men are created equal and that they have the right to equal justice under law. We believe that all men have the right to freedom of thought and of expression and the right to worship as they please. We believe that all men are entitled to equal opportunities for jobs, for homes, for good health and for education. We believe that all men should have a voice in their government and that government should protect, not usurp, the rights of the people. These are the basic civil rights, which are the source and the support of our democracy.

Unfortunately, many people disagreed with Truman’s views. Truman’s approval ratings went down, and few members of the white population supported his objectives. Despite this, Truman did keep his word. Before he left office, changes were made to America’s civil rights policies. In July of 1948, racial discrimination was officially banned from federal government hiring practices, and Truman ordered an end to military segregation. Truman’s advocacy of civil rights helped to spark the civil rights movement that was later to come. Over the next sixty years, America’s views on civil rights and equality dramatically changed, and the actions Truman took as president had much to do with this.

Foreign Affairs

Beyond being successful in leading America, Truman was also regarded as extremely successful in foreign affairs. One of the most famous foreign policies Truman is known for is NATO (National Atlantic Treaty Organization). This was a peacetime military alliance that sought to maintain peace among nations. The members who signed NATO’s treaty agreed to help to protect all other nations involved. An attack against one of the allied nations would be considered an attack against all nations that were parties to the treaty. The original twelve nations to sign the treaty were Great Britain, France, Italy, the Netherlands, Belgium, Luxembourg, Norway, Denmark, Portugal, Iceland, Canada, and the United States. America signed the treaty in 1949, hoping it could help to counter the U.S.S.R.’s military power.

Truman also worked on pre-existing foreign policies, such as the National Security Team he inherited from Roosevelt. As leader of the National Security Team (NST), Truman’s job was to handle the negotiations at the post-war conferences of allied foreign ministers. Throughout Truman’s presidency, he fired and brought several leaders of the NST into place. Truman first replaced FDR's last secretary of state, Edward Stettinius, with former senator, Supreme Court justice and war mobilization director James F. Byrnes, who was later replaced with Gen. George C. Marshall. In 1947, the NST passed the National Security Act, reorganizing the military. This act brought together the Department of War and the Department of the Navy, turning them into the National Military Establishment, which was to be headed by the Secretary of Defense. The NST is also responsible for the creation of the Central Intelligence Agency (CIA) in 1947. The CIA is meant to gather intelligence about foreign affairs, including facts regarding governments, corporations, and persons. The NST, in addition, established the National Security Council (NSC) to advise the president about issues related to American foreign policy, and this council became powerful during the Korean War. Overall, the NST was a great success, creating important organizations and policies that are still with us today.

After World War II, much of Europe was left in ruins. Because of this, both the United States and Soviet Union were scrambling to gain influence over the weakened areas. America was committed to halting the spread of communism, so Truman created the Marshall Plan. The Marshall Plan was meant to aid in the reconstruction of Europe after the war. Overall, America spent over $13 billion rebuilding other countries under the terms of the Marshall Plan. Truman also took action to stop the spread of the Soviet Union’s influence by enacting the Truman Doctrine, which was introduced to prevent the spread of communism. This battle for dominance between the United States and the Soviet Union was one of the leading factors in the beginning of the Cold War. Truman hoped to maintain peace with the Soviet Union through negotiations and compromise. Unfortunately, nothing could subdue the rivalry, and the Cold War ended up lasting from 1947 to 1991, long past Truman’s presidency.

Conclusion

To this day, many people believe Truman to be one of the five greatest presidents in America’s history. He accomplished a great amount within a very short period of time. It is true that not all of his proposals were accepted and not all his policies went into effect, but every reform he tried to undertake has left a mark on America. When I think about what it truly means to be an American I look to Harry Truman - a true patriot who fought for the freedom, stability, and equality of his country and its citizens.
